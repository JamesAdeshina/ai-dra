The rehabilitation gap

Recovery should not become invisible once a survivor returns home.

Home exercise can be difficult to perform consistently when guidance, reassurance, and visibility of progress are limited.

01Exercise at homeOften without immediate feedback on movement quality.
02Progress becomes hard to seeSurvivors and carers rely on memory or isolated appointments.
03Engagement data is lostResearchers and care teams cannot easily understand what happens between sessions.
One connected response

AI-DRA turns a home exercise session into guided, measurable rehabilitation.

The platform is being designed to support the whole rehabilitation relationship, not only the movement on camera.

See and respond to movement

Live pose landmarks, joint angles, hold timing, repetitions, and rule-based feedback help make each exercise more understandable.

Keep carers meaningfully connected

Permission-based linking lets carers support routines, review progress, and receive relevant notifications without taking over the survivor's experience.

Make engagement visible for research

A focused monitoring layer can show who is engaging, which exercises are completed, where sessions are abandoned, and how performance changes over time.

A complete rehabilitation ecosystem

Three experiences. One shared picture of progress.

AI-DRA is differentiated by the way its interfaces work together around the survivor, rather than operating as isolated tools.

Clear guidance before, during, and after every session.

The survivor journey is designed to reduce cognitive load, make exercises easier to follow, and turn progress into something visible and encouraging.

  • Accessible authentication, dashboard, exercise library, and profile settings.
  • Exercise demonstrations, step-by-step preparation, and live camera guidance.
  • Repetition counting, hold timing, session history, progress, and reminders.
  • Optional voice instructions, sound effects, and reduced-motion preferences.

Why AI-DRA stands apart

More than exercise videos. More practical than specialised hardware.

AI-DRA's strongest advantage is the combination of accessible computer vision, connected support, and research-focused data in a single prototype.

01

Uses devices people already have

No wearable sensor setup, charging routine, or specialised camera is required for the intended experience.

02

Responds to movement, not only completion

The system is being built around joint angles, range of motion, holds, repetitions, speed, and movement quality signals.

03

Connects the rehabilitation network

Survivors, carers, and researchers each receive a purpose-built view instead of sharing one generic dashboard.

04

Built to support evaluation

Session, attempt, completion, engagement, and movement records can support quantitative analysis and future usability studies.

Movement intelligence

From camera landmarks to rehabilitation feedback.

AI-DRA does not need to store every camera frame. The system can translate live movement into smaller, meaningful rehabilitation measures and session records.

01

Detect body and hand landmarks

MediaPipe-based tracking identifies the relevant shoulder, elbow, wrist, and hand points.

02

Interpret the exercise state

Joint angles, position thresholds, timing, and movement stages determine whether a repetition is progressing correctly.

03

Guide the survivor live

On-screen and optional spoken prompts explain when to raise, hold, return, slow down, or adjust.

04

Summarise the session

Completion, accuracy, speed, range of motion, holds, and attempt states can contribute to a movement score and recommendation.

Research-led by design

Built to learn from real use, not only to look impressive in a demonstration.

AI-DRA is a seed-funded research prototype. Its purpose is to explore how digital rehabilitation can be accessible, usable, and measurable for stroke survivors and the people supporting them.

Privacy-aware data design

Prioritise meaningful movement and session measures rather than storing unnecessary raw camera data.

Co-design and usability focus

Survivor, carer, and healthcare professional feedback can directly shape wording, interactions, accessibility, and workflow.

Transparent prototype evaluation

Separate validated functionality from features still under development, then test both usability and technical performance.

The survivor journey

Designed to make each session feel clear, supported, and achievable.

The interface reduces unnecessary steps and guides the survivor from preparation to reflection.

01

Choose an exercise

Browse assigned or recommended upper-limb and functional activities.

02

Prepare safely

Review simple instructions, positioning, camera setup, and audio preferences.

03

Move with guidance

Follow visual and optional spoken prompts while the system tracks the exercise.

04

Review progress

See session completion, movement measures, history, and the suggested next step.
